Electromagnetic Induction

Electromagnetic induction is defined as the production of voltage across an electric conductor in a changing magnetic field or when the conductor moves through a stationary magnetic field, resulting in the generation of electric current. This happens because when a conductor is placed in a varying magnetic field, an electromotive force is induced.

The currents captured in this phenomenon can be used as electricity. This process is called induction because there is no physical connection between the inductor and the magnet.

To induce the voltage you can move a magnet toward or away from the coil or rotate the coil within a magnetic field.

You can also increase the voltage by increasing the amount of wire or wire turns because it increases the total amount of conductor length cutting through the magnetic field, multiplying the effect.

Electromagnetic induction converts mechanical energy into electrical energy by rotating a coil in a magnetic field.

Faraday's Law: The induced electromotive force is proportional to the rate of the change of magnetic flux through the coil. This happens because of the conservation of energy and the nature of magnetic force on electrons. A faster change in magnetic flux through a coil creates a stronger magnetic force on electrons.

Faraday's First Law: Whenever there is a change in the magnetic flux passing through the coil of a wire, an electromotive force (emf) is induced in the coil.
